PG3D: Programmatic Geometric Guidance for 3D Diffusion Policies

A self-contained proposal for constraint-compositional robot manipulation in ManiSkill

Draft date: May 17, 2026 · Scope: simulation-only research MVP, with robot-agnostic software interfaces

One-sentence thesis: Executable geometric programs can provide an explicit test-time constraint interface for 3D diffusion policies, and kinematic point-cloud rollouts can make those constraints actionable before execution by imagining how the robot geometry will move through the observed scene.
Key update from the earlier draft: ManiSkill/SAPIEN is now the central simulator. The first MVP is a controlled reach task, followed by manipulation tasks such as pick, stack, and placement. Real-robot implementation is out of scope for the current plan, but the interfaces remain robot-agnostic.

1. Executive summary

This project studies how to compose learned 3D robot policies with explicit geometric constraints at inference time. A nominal 3D diffusion policy can learn useful reaching and manipulation behaviors from demonstrations, but it may not obey deployment-time constraints that were absent from the training data: avoid a newly introduced keep-out region, do not pass over a restricted zone, approach from a specified side, maintain smooth robot motion, or place an object in relation to another object.

Pure geometric planners and code-as-policy systems can express these constraints explicitly, but they can discard the learned manipulation prior that makes imitation policies robust in clutter, partial observability, contact-rich execution, and perceptual ambiguity. PG3D combines these strengths: a 3D diffusion policy proposes action chunks; a geometric program scores what should be true; and a kinematic point-cloud world model predicts how candidate actions would move the robot through the current scene before execution.

The first proof-of-concept is deliberately simple: train a DP3-style point-cloud diffusion policy on nominal ManiSkill reach demonstrations, then add a new avoid-region constraint only at evaluation time. The policy samples multiple action chunks. For each candidate, PG3D imagines the future robot geometry in point-cloud space, evaluates the handwritten geometric program, reranks candidates, and executes the best chunk in receding-horizon mode.

After constrained reach works, the same machinery extends to pick-and-place, stack, and placement tasks. The core research question is whether programmatic geometric constraints can steer learned 3D diffusion policies without retraining, while preserving enough of the learned prior to outperform or complement purely code-only geometry baselines.

2. Motivation and problem setting

2.1 Deployment-time constraints

Consider a robot policy trained from nominal demonstrations. At deployment time, the robot may be asked to satisfy extra constraints that were not explicitly demonstrated. Examples include:

  • Avoid a keep-out sphere, box, or tabletop region near the nominal path.
  • Do not let the gripper, end effector, or carried object pass over a restricted zone.
  • Approach a target from a specified direction or cone.
  • Place an object left of, behind, between, or inside other objects.
  • Maintain gripper or object orientation during transport.
  • Satisfy robot-specific feasibility constraints such as joint limits, velocity limits, collision, and workspace bounds.

2.2 Mathematical view

Let p_theta(tau | P_t, s_t) denote a 3D diffusion policy over an action chunk tau, conditioned on the current point cloud P_t and robot state s_t. Let C denote an executable geometric constraint program. Earlier formulations score constraints directly on sampled trajectories. PG3D adds an intermediate object: an imagined rollout generated by a kinematic point-cloud world model. 

candidate action chunk:   tau ~ p_theta(tau | P_t, s_t)
kinematic world model:    R_tau = G(P_t, robot_mask_t, q_t, tau)
constraint program:       E_C(R_tau), F_C(R_tau)
composed selection:       argmin_tau [E_C(R_tau) + E_task(R_tau) + E_prior_proxy(tau)]

Here R_tau contains the predicted joint sequence, end-effector path, future robot point clouds, composited scene point clouds, and masks. The constraint program can then evaluate clearance, smoothness, no-over-zone costs, and feasibility proxies before the action is executed.

3. Why ManiSkill is the primary simulator

ManiSkill is a SAPIEN-based robotics simulation and training framework with a Gym/Gymnasium interface, support for reinforcement learning and imitation learning workflows, GPU/CPU simulation, and fast parallelized rendering. It is particularly well matched to PG3D because the project is centered on point-cloud observations, segmentation masks, custom task variants, and controlled geometry overlays.

Point-cloud first

ManiSkill supports visual observation modes such as rgb+depth+segmentation and pointcloud, which directly match the input representation needed for 3D diffusion policies and robot-mask compositing.

Custom task control

The first milestone needs tight control over start and goal distributions. ManiSkill’s task-building API is a better fit for creating PG3DReach-v0, constrained reach variants, and later pick/place overlays.

GPU throughput

ManiSkill v3 emphasizes GPU-parallelized simulation, rendering, and heterogeneous scene execution, which is useful for data collection and later sample-count ablations.

Manipulation ladder

The platform already exposes manipulation tasks such as PickCube-v1, StackCube-v1, and PegInsertionSide-v1, giving a natural path from reach to contact-rich manipulation.

Visualization and masks

ManiSkill includes example tools for point-cloud and segmentation visualization, which aligns with the need to inspect imagined rollouts, selected trajectories, and constraint violations.

Related baseline fit

Because ManiSkill is SAPIEN-based, it is closer to several modern 3D manipulation codebases and easier to customize for CodeDiffuser-style or obstacle-guidance baselines later.

The main caveat is that ManiSkill v3 is still a fast-moving stack. This is acceptable for PG3D because the first MVP is narrow, custom, and simulation-only. The implementation should pin versions, keep the environment adapter isolated, and document exact commands in the repository runbooks.

4. Related work and research gap

4.1 3D diffusion and point-cloud robot policies

DP3 shows that sparse point-cloud representations can be paired with diffusion policies to learn visuomotor control across many simulation and real-world tasks. RISE and 3D Diffuser Actor similarly motivate 3D representations for robot imitation. These works provide strong learned priors, but they do not by themselves expose a typed, executable interface for new test-time geometric constraints.

4.2 Inference-time policy steering

ITPS, value-guided policy steering, and policy-composition methods study ways to steer generative policies at inference time. PG3D is most closely aligned with this family but makes a specific commitment: the steering signal is an executable geometric program evaluated over imagined 3D robot rollouts.

4.3 Code-as-policy and geometric-constraint systems

ReKep, GeoManip, and code-as-policy systems demonstrate that executable geometric constraints can express rich manipulation structure. PG3D differs by using code as a constraint interface over a learned diffusion-policy distribution, not as the entire controller.

4.4 Neural motion planning and reactive policies

NeuralMP and Deep Reactive Policy show that point-cloud conditioned motion policies, procedural obstacle scenes, and motion-planning supervision are productive for collision-aware reaching and motion generation. PG3D borrows the emphasis on point clouds, joint-space motion, and robot geometry, but studies a different composition question: how to steer a diffusion policy with newly supplied executable constraints at test time.

4.5 Diffusion guidance and executable attention

Lan-o3DP, GravMAD, AffordDP, and CodeDiffuser use obstacle costs, grounded spatial maps, affordance guidance, or VLM-generated code/attention to guide action generation. PG3D focuses on trajectory-level executable programs and the ability to evaluate constraints through imagined robot-geometry rollouts.

Related directionRepresentative worksGap addressed by PG3D
3D diffusion policiesDP3, RISE, 3D Diffuser ActorStrong 3D policy priors, but no general executable constraint interface.
Inference-time steeringITPS, value-guided and distribution-composition methodsSteering exists, but not centered on typed geometric programs over imagined robot point clouds.
Code/geometric solversReKep, GeoManip, code-as-policy systemsExpressive constraints, but often use code/optimization as the controller rather than as guidance over a learned prior.
Neural motion planningNeuralMP, Deep Reactive PolicyRelevant for reach and collision-aware motion, but not primarily about diffusion-policy constraint composition.
3D diffusion guidanceLan-o3DP, GravMAD, AffordDP, CodeDiffuserGuidance via maps, attention, affordance, or obstacle cost; less emphasis on composable trajectory programs and hard checks.

5. Proposed method

5.1 System overview

ManiSkill observation
  -> point cloud + robot state + robot/object masks
  -> DP3-style diffusion policy samples K action chunks
  -> kinematic point-cloud world model imagines each candidate
  -> executable geometric programs score imagined rollouts
  -> rejection / reranking / receding-horizon execution
  -> later: energy-guided denoising + final reranking

5.2 Observation and action representation

The policy should operate on point clouds and robot state. Simulator ground truth may be used for evaluation, constraint-instance generation, visualization, and debugging, but should not be required as policy input. The initial action representation should be joint-space action chunks, with end-effector trajectories recovered through forward kinematics.

Design choicePreferred P0 optionRationale
ObservationManiSkill point cloud + robot proprioception; optional segmentation masksMatches 3D diffusion policies and enables robot-mask point-cloud compositing.
Action chunksAbsolute joint target chunks, with delta joint chunks as fallbackForward kinematics becomes direct; no IK is required to imagine robot geometry.
PolicyDP3-style sparse point-cloud diffusion policyBest match for the 3D point-cloud diffusion-policy thesis.
Control loopReceding-horizon action chunks, about one second per chunk initiallyAllows re-observation, repeated sampling, and multi-step imagination.

5.3 Kinematic point-cloud world model

The world model is the central new component. It should initially be deterministic and geometry-based rather than learned. Given the current point cloud, robot mask, robot joint state, and a candidate action chunk, it predicts how the robot geometry would appear in future point-cloud observations. 

Inputs:
  P_t                  current point cloud
  M_robot_t            robot mask in current point cloud
  q_t                  current joint state
  tau                  candidate joint action chunk
  robot_model          URDF / link meshes / FK model

Outputs:
  q_{t:t+H}            predicted joint sequence
  ee_{t:t+H}           end-effector path
  robot_cloud_{t:t+H}  sampled future robot geometry
  P_hat_{t:t+H}        composited future scene point clouds
  masks_hat            future robot masks and optional proxy masks

The P0 compositor is simple: remove current robot points from the observed point cloud, sample the robot mesh at future joint states, and insert future robot points into the static scene. In reach, this is sufficient because no object dynamics are needed. In pick-and-place, the world model later adds a gripper proxy and then a carried-object proxy or attached object point cloud.

5.4 Constraint-program representation

The first interface should be Python-first, with JSON-serializable configuration for replayable experiments. A full DSL is unnecessary for the MVP. 

class Constraint:
    def cost(self, rollout: ImaginedRollout, scene: SceneContext) -> dict[str, float]:
        ...

    def satisfied(self, rollout: ImaginedRollout, scene: SceneContext) -> bool:
        ...
PrimitiveP0/P1Definition
avoid_regionP0Penalize the EEF, gripper proxy, robot-link points, or carried-object proxy for entering a sphere, box, cylinder, or other simple keep-out primitive.
avoid_projectionP0.5Reach analog of no-overflight: penalize the XY projection of the EEF or gripper for passing over a restricted tabletop region.
smoothnessP0Penalize jagged joint or end-effector trajectories.
avoid_overflightP1Manipulation version of no-over-zone: penalize a carried-object proxy for passing over a restricted object or region.
place_relativeP2Evaluate final object relation, such as left-of, behind, between, or inside.
approach_fromP2Evaluate the final pre-grasp approach segment against an approach cone or direction.

P0 should avoid heavy hard constraints. The only hard-style checks should be simple kinematic feasibility: joint limits, action bounds, and optionally velocity limits. Collision, IK, and whole-arm feasibility checks are important but should be added after the basic steering/reranking loop works.

5.5 Composition operators

The MVP should implement candidate reranking before energy guidance. Reranking works with arbitrary Python checks, visualization, and non-differentiable logic. 

candidates = policy.sample_action_chunks(obs, K)
for tau in candidates:
    rollout = world_model.imagine(obs, tau)
    costs = constraint_program.cost(rollout, scene)
    score = - weighted_sum(costs) - task_cost(rollout) + prior_proxy(tau)
execute(best_tau)

The initial prior proxy can be lightweight: smoothness, action magnitude, and deviation from the sample consensus. A DP3 denoising-score proxy or diffusion likelihood surrogate can be added later, but should not block the first MVP.

6. Task ladder and experiment plan

6.1 Task ladder

StageEnvironmentPurpose
Stage APG3DReach-Narrow-v0Debug observation adapters, action chunks, DP3 training, and deterministic start/goal distributions.
Stage BPG3DReach-Medium-v0First real constrained-reach evaluation with unseen keep-out regions.
Stage CPG3DReach-ConstrainedEval-v0Evaluate avoid-region and no-over-zone constraints without constrained training demos.
Stage DPickCube-v1 or narrow custom pick taskMove from motion-only reach to object manipulation.
Stage EStackCube-v1, placement, or insertion taskTest carried-object constraints, relational placement, and more contact-sensitive behavior.

6.2 Experiment 1: nominal reach sanity check

Train a DP3-style policy on nominal ManiSkill reach demonstrations. Evaluate reach success, final target distance, smoothness, and action-chunk stability. This experiment establishes whether the base policy is good enough to steer.

6.3 Experiment 2: constrained reach with avoid-region

Train only on nominal reach. At evaluation time, place a simple keep-out sphere or box near the nominal path. Compare base policy, rejection/filtering, world-model reranking, code-only waypoint planning, and a simple ITPS-style sample-and-rank baseline. The main outcome is combined reach-and-constraint success.

6.4 Experiment 3: no-over-zone reach analog

Evaluate a tabletop projection constraint where the EEF or gripper proxy should not pass over a restricted 2D region. This validates the geometric form of no-overflight before introducing grasped objects.

6.5 Experiment 4: pick-and-place transfer

Move to a narrow pick task or PickCube-v1. Add a gripper proxy and later an object proxy to the world model. Evaluate whether the same reranking machinery improves constraint satisfaction without destroying task success.

6.6 Experiment 5: carried-object overflight and placement

After pick-and-place works, implement a carried-object proxy and evaluate no-overflight constraints. Later add relative placement and approach-direction constraints to test multi-constraint composition.

9. Risks and success criteria

9.1 Technical risks

RiskMitigation
ManiSkill version churn complicates setup.Pin versions, isolate the adapter, keep exact uv commands and runbooks up to date, and avoid unnecessary simulator patches.
Custom reach is too easy and code-only planning dominates.Use reach only as a mechanism-validation task. Move quickly to pick and place once the machinery works.
DP3 adaptation takes longer than expected.Keep a minimal dataset/action adapter first; debug with small reach data; avoid adding language, multitask, or real-robot scope.
Reranking cannot find feasible samples.Increase K from 16 to 32 to 64; improve sample diversity; add energy guidance later if feasible samples are too rare.
World model is inaccurate for manipulation.Start with robot-only reach; then add gripper proxy; then carried-object primitive; only later attach object point clouds.
Lack of novelty over simple steering baselines.Make the world-model ablation central: current-chunk-only vs robot-point-cloud rollout vs multi-chunk imagined rollout vs object-proxy rollout.

9.2 First MVP success criteria

  1. The nominal DP3-style policy solves controlled ManiSkill reach at a usable rate.
  2. The base policy sometimes violates an unseen evaluation-time avoid-region constraint.
  3. The kinematic point-cloud world model predicts candidate EEF/robot paths well enough to identify likely violations before execution.
  4. World-model reranking improves combined reach-and-constraint success over the base policy and simple rejection/filtering.
  5. Visualizations clearly show point clouds, candidate trajectories, keep-out regions, selected candidates, and failure modes.

9.3 Paper-scale success criteria

  1. The method transfers from reach to pick-and-place and at least one placement-style manipulation task.
  2. The method improves combined task-and-constraint success under constraints not present in training demonstrations.
  3. The learned-policy prior still matters: code-only waypoint planning is strong on reach but brittle or incomplete on manipulation tasks.
  4. The world-model contribution is empirically supported through ablations.
  5. LLM-generated programs can instantiate the validated constraint primitives, but the main result does not depend on LLM reliability.
